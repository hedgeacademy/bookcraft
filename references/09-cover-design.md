# Cover Design and Commercial Packaging

Author: Xinyi Chen, founder of HEDGE Global. Chinese introduction: 陈歆怡，海聚海外 CEO。Contact: `chenxinyi_g`.

Use this module when the user asks for cover direction, title packaging, subtitle sharpening, sales copy, book positioning for shelves or thumbnails, or a prompt-ready design brief.

The goal is not to make a pretty cover in isolation. The goal is to make the book instantly legible as a commercial object: the reader should know what the book is, why it matters, and why it belongs to them within a few seconds.

## Core Principle

A strong commercial cover usually answers four questions quickly:

- What is the book about?
- Who is it for?
- What emotional or practical promise does it make?
- Why should this specific book be picked instead of adjacent books?

If the cover cannot answer those questions at thumbnail size, revise the hierarchy before adding decoration.

## Inputs to Collect

Before proposing cover directions, extract or ask for:

- book title and subtitle
- target reader
- category and comparable books
- core promise
- emotional tone
- strongest image, metaphor, object, or scene in the manuscript
- author identity or authority signal
- sales channel: bookstore shelf, online thumbnail, social media, course/community handoff, or private client delivery

If information is missing, infer conservative defaults and mark assumptions.

## Cover Positioning Formula

Summarize the cover strategy as:

```text
For [target reader], this book promises [reader outcome] through [core method/viewpoint], expressed visually by [symbol/metaphor].
```

Then derive:

- one-sentence sales hook
- primary visual symbol
- title hierarchy
- subtitle role
- author/credential placement
- color and typography direction
- shelf/thumbnail readability check

## The 3-Second Shelf Test

Evaluate every direction with a 3-second test:

- At first glance, can the reader identify the category?
- Is the title readable before the subtitle?
- Does the cover have one dominant visual idea?
- Does the subtitle sharpen the promise instead of repeating the title?
- Is there enough contrast for online thumbnails?
- Would the cover still make sense without reading back-cover copy?

If a direction fails the test, simplify.

## Hierarchy Rules

Prioritize the cover in this order:

1. **Title**: the biggest meaning carrier; must be readable small.
2. **Visual symbol**: one strong idea, not a collage of every chapter.
3. **Subtitle**: clarifies reader benefit, method, or category.
4. **Author signal**: name, identity, credential, or endorsement only when it helps trust.
5. **Series or imprint mark**: optional and visually secondary.

Avoid making all elements equally loud. A cover should have a clear lead actor.

## Title and Subtitle Checks

For title packaging:

- Prefer specific, memorable, searchable wording.
- Avoid generic abstract titles unless the visual symbol is unusually strong.
- Make the category clear when the title is poetic.
- Keep the subtitle short and functional.
- Do not use the subtitle to explain the entire table of contents.

For Chinese commercial nonfiction, a useful subtitle often names one of:

- the reader transformation
- the method
- the scenario
- the pain point
- the practical outcome

## Visual Direction Patterns

Use these patterns as starting points, not templates.

### 1. Object as Concept

Use one concrete object to represent the book's core idea. Best for business, finance, productivity, memoir, and social observation.

Output should include:

- object
- symbolic meaning
- composition
- color
- title placement

### 2. Tension Scene

Show a visual conflict or pressure point. Best for transformation, entrepreneurship, career, family, education, and narrative nonfiction.

Output should include:

- scene
- tension
- emotional temperature
- reader identification point
- typography mood

### 3. System Diagram

Turn the book's method into a clean visual structure. Best for frameworks, technical books, management books, and training material.

Output should include:

- core structure
- number of parts
- visual grammar
- chart/diagram restraint
- readability rules

### 4. Author as Signal

Use the author's identity when personal credibility, memoir value, or founder/operator experience is central.

Output should include:

- author role
- portrait or non-portrait choice
- trust signal
- avoided vanity signals
- cover copy balance

### 5. Strong Type Cover

Use typography as the main visual. Best when the title is exceptionally strong and the category is already clear.

Output should include:

- type style
- title rhythm
- color contrast
- spacing
- thumbnail behavior

## Color and Typography Guidance

Choose colors by market signal, not personal preference.

- Business and operations: restrained contrast, clear hierarchy, limited palette.
- Finance and risk: high contrast, controlled tension, avoid decorative luxury cliches.
- Self-growth and psychology: warmth plus clarity, avoid soft indistinct gradients.
- Technology and AI: clean structure, sharp contrast, avoid generic blue-purple futurism unless differentiated.
- Memoir and narrative nonfiction: emotional specificity, one memorable image, quieter typography.

Typography should match the promise:

- practical method: stable, legible, structured
- sharp argument: high contrast, bold weight
- literary nonfiction: elegant but readable
- founder/operator story: confident, unfussy
- course-like handbook: clean, modular, instructional

## Image Prompt Rules

When writing image prompts:

- Specify book-cover aspect ratio, usually vertical.
- State that the title area must be clean and readable.
- Use one dominant image or symbol.
- Avoid asking the image model to render exact Chinese text unless the tool is known to handle it well; reserve title/subtitle as editable typography when possible.
- Include negative guidance: no crowded collage, no tiny text, no fake publisher logos, no fake awards, no unreadable type.

## Review Checklist

Before finalizing a cover direction, check:

- title reads first
- category is clear
- visual idea is singular
- subtitle adds value
- no fake endorsements or awards
- no fake publisher or imprint marks
- no overpromised claims unsupported by the manuscript
- thumbnail remains readable
- cover promise matches the book outline and first chapters
